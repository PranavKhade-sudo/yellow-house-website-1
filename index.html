History of the Tiranga

The Indian national flag, popularly known as the Tiranga (meaning “tricolour”), is a symbol of pride, unity, and freedom for the nation. It consists of three horizontal stripes: saffron at the top, white in the middle, and green at the bottom, with a navy blue Ashoka Chakra (wheel) with 24 spokes in the centre.

The flag was designed by Pingali Venkayya, a freedom fighter and agriculturist from Andhra Pradesh. It was officially adopted on 22 July 1947, shortly before India gained independence from British rule.

The colours of the flag carry deep meanings:

  • Saffron: Courage and sacrifice.
  • White: Truth, peace, and purity.
  • Green: Prosperity and fertility of the land.
  • Ashoka Chakra: The eternal wheel of law (Dharma) and progress.

Before the current design, India had several other versions of the national flag during the freedom movement. Early versions in 1906 featured stripes of green, yellow, and red, while later ones incorporated the spinning wheel (charkha) symbol inspired by Mahatma Gandhi’s call for self-reliance.

The flag’s proportions are 3:2, and its use is governed by the Indian Flag Code. It must be treated with the utmost respect, never touching the ground or used for commercial purposes.

The Tiranga is a unifying emblem for all Indians, representing the sacrifices made during the struggle for independence and the values on which the Republic is built.

Great Social Reformers of India

Mahatma Gandhi

Leader of India’s freedom movement, known for his philosophy of non-violence (Ahimsa) and civil disobedience.

B. R. Ambedkar

Architect of the Indian Constitution and champion of social justice and equality for marginalized communities.

Savitribai Phule

Pioneer of women's education in India and a social reformer who fought against caste and gender discrimination.

Swami Vivekananda

Spiritual leader and philosopher who spread the message of Vedanta and inspired youth towards service and nation-building.
